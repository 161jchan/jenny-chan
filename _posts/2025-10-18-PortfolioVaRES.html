Phase 1: Building the Daily PnL Vector

Our first task is to construct the historical Profit and Loss ($\text{PnL}$) vector for the entire portfolio. We do this by calculating the daily dollar change for each position and then summing them up. **The sign is everything here!**

The Position PnL Formula

For each day \(t\), the dollar PnL for an individual asset is:

$$ \text{PnL}_{\text{Asset}, t} = (\text{Price}_t - \text{Price}_{t-1}) \times \text{Signed Quantity} $$

The total portfolio PnL ($\text{PnL}_t$) is the sum of these components:

$$ \text{PnL}_t = \text{PnL}_{\text{TSLA}, t} + \text{PnL}_{\text{NVDA}, t} + \text{PnL}_{\text{SPY}, t} $$

The Crucial Short Position Logic

The **TSLA** and **NVDA** positions are easy: Long means a positive quantity (+$100$ and +$200$). But for the **Short 50 shares of SPY**, we must use a **negative quantity** to correctly capture the PnL:

$$ \text{PnL}_{\text{SPY}, t} = (\text{SPY}_t - \text{SPY}_{t-1}) \times \mathbf{(-50)} $$

If SPY's price goes up, the price change is positive, but multiplying by \(-50\) correctly results in a **negative PnL** (a loss) for our short position. This PnL vector forms the core of our risk analysis.

Phase 2: Calculating the $\mathbf{99\%}$ VaR (The Loss Threshold)

The $99\%$ VaR is the loss amount that is only exceeded $1\%$ of the time. In the Historical Simulation method, this is equivalent to finding the **$1^{\text{st}}$ percentile** (or $0.01$ quantile) of our daily PnL vector.

Assuming our daily PnL data is in the Excel range B2:B505 (representing the ~500 days of data):

$$ \mathbf{99\%\ VaR} = \mathbf{ABS}(\text{PERCENTILE.INC}(\text{B2:B505}, \mathbf{0.01})) $$

The use of PERCENTILE.INC(..., 0.01) finds the largest negative value that separates the worst $1\%$ of outcomes. We wrap it in ABS() because VaR is conventionally reported as a **positive dollar loss**.

Phase 3: Uncovering the Expected Shortfall (The Average Tail Loss)

Expected Shortfall (ES) is a superior risk metric because it tells us what we can expect to lose *if* a VaR breach actually occurs. It is simply the average of all losses that are worse (more negative) than the VaR threshold.

The Two-Step ES Calculation

  1. First, we define our negative VaR threshold (let's say we store it in cell C1): $$ \mathbf{VaR}_{\text{Threshold}} = \text{PERCENTILE.INC}(\text{B2:B505}, 0.01) $$
  2. Next, we use the powerful AVERAGEIF function to find the average of all PnL values that are strictly less than this threshold: $$ \mathbf{99\%\ ES} = \mathbf{ABS}(\text{AVERAGEIF}(\text{B2:B505}, "\mathbf{<}" \& \mathbf{C1})) $$

This final number represents the average loss we would expect on the worst $1\%$ of days. Itâ€™s a vital piece of information for any serious risk manager!
